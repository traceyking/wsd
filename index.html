What to Pack

You should always pack just incase an emergency should come up in your journey or if your vehicle breaks down.

  • Pack clothes according to weather conditions to your destination
  • Basic toiletries
  • Camping gear if you are planing on stoping at campgrounds. Such as sleeping bag tent small camp stove
  • Snacks and plenty of water
  • First Aid kit
  • Car emergency kit

Where to sleep along the way

  • Decide wether you want to stay in hotels the whole trip or if you want to find campgrounds along the way.
  • Make sure you research campgrounds and find out cost per night, amenities such as showers, fire wood, electric and local wildlife to watch out for.
  • If your staying in primarily hotels you should look to find the best deal and if you need a reservation prior.

What sites are along the way

Make a list of places you would like to stop along your route. It could the Worlds Largest Ball of Yarnor a national landmark.

  • National Landmarks
  • Museums
  • Historical Sites
  • Beaches
  • Theme Parks
  • Activities

What to Eat

  • Look up local cuisine restaurants
  • Plan a picknick in the park or at a scenic spot.
  • Do a search to see what is off each exit

How much with the trip cost

  • Create a Budget unless you have an endless supply of funds.
  • Make a list of how many days and nights you will be on your trip then figure out the cost of hotels or other sleeping arrangements.
  • Create a daily estimate of food cost
  • How much will it cost you in fuel. Figure out your miles traveled and your vehicle's estimated MPG and cost of gas per gallon.
  • How much will the sites and activities cost you
  • How much do plan on spending souvenirs and other miscellaneous items.
  • Write down all of your calculations and add it up. Then add in at least 15% more for emergencies. Things always cost more that expected.

Once you've got it all planned out load the car and hit the road for a journey of a lifetime!

Featured Destination

The Grand Canyon The Canyon is an overwhelming experience, and nothing can prepare a visitor for the sight. The Grand Canyon is a massive canyon carved over several million years by the Colorado River. Grand Canyon National Park boasts an elevation change of nearly 7,000 feet (2130 m) from Point Imperial (at nearly 9,000 feet or 2740 m) to the banks of Lake Mead (at just over 2,000 feet or 610 m). The canyon itself is, from rim to river over a mile (1610 m) deep. In spots the rock layers exposed in the canyon display over two billion years of geologic history.
